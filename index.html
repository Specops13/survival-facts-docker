Water

Daily Requirements

The human body needs approximately 2–3 liters of water per day at rest in temperate conditions. Heavy exertion, heat, or altitude can double or triple this requirement. Dehydration impairs judgment long before it impairs physical performance.

Activity LevelEstimated NeedPriority
Rest / cool climate1.5–2 L/dayModerate
Light activity2–3 L/dayHigh
Heavy exertion / heat4–6 L/dayCritical

Signs of Dehydration

  • Mild (1–2%): thirst, darker urine, slight headache
  • Moderate (3–5%): fatigue, dizziness, reduced urine output, dry mouth
  • Severe (6%+): confusion, rapid heartbeat, inability to stand — medical emergency
Urine color is your best field indicator. Pale straw = hydrated. Dark amber = drink immediately. No output for 8+ hours = critical.

Finding Water

  • Follow terrain downhill — water collects in valleys, gullies, and canyon floors. Animal trails converging downhill often lead to a source.
  • Dew collection: Before sunrise, wipe dew from grass or leaves with an absorbent cloth, then wring it into a container. A single morning can yield 500 mL or more.
  • Transpiration bag: Tie a clear plastic bag around leafy branches in sunlight. Moisture transpired by the plant collects in the corner of the bag.
  • Solar still: Dig a pit (~1 m wide, 50 cm deep), place container at center, cover with clear plastic weighted at edges and center with a rock. Moisture condenses on the underside and drips into the container. Output is low (200–500 mL/day) — best used with green vegetation placed inside the pit.
  • Rocky crevices: After rain, water pools in shaded rock depressions and can be extracted with cloth or tubing.
  • Indicators of groundwater: dense green vegetation, willows, cottonwoods, cattails, or insects (especially mosquitoes and bees) flying in a persistent direction.
Never drink seawater. Never drink urine. The salt content causes your kidneys to use more water than you consume, accelerating dehydration. Cactus fluid (except barrel cactus in small amounts) is highly acidic and will cause vomiting.

Fire

The Fire Triangle

Every fire requires heat + fuel + oxygen. Removing any one element extinguishes it. Building a fire is a progression from smallest to largest fuel: tinder → kindling → fuel wood. Never skip steps.

Site Preparation

  • Choose a spot sheltered from wind but with airflow — avoid dense canopy overhead.
  • Clear a 1-meter radius of debris down to bare earth or rock.
  • In snow or wet ground, build a platform of green logs to insulate the fire from the ground.
  • Have twice as much tinder and kindling ready before striking a single spark.

Tinder, Kindling & Fuel

StageExamplesNotes
TinderDry grass, birch bark, fatwood shavings, cattail fluff, dried fungi, char cloth, fine bark fibersMust ignite from a spark or brief flame. Prepare a palm-sized bundle.
KindlingDry sticks pencil-to-finger thickness, split wood, dry pine needlesAdd after tinder is established. Cross-hatch for airflow.
FuelWrist-to-arm thickness dry hardwood, standing dead woodAdd once kindling is burning steadily. Hardwood burns longer; softwood ignites easier.
Birch bark contains oils that ignite even when damp. Fatwood (resin-saturated pine, identified by heavy weight and strong pine smell) is the best natural firestarter. Dead wood still attached to the tree is nearly always drier than wood on the ground.

Fire Starting Methods

  • Ferrocerium rod: Strike at a low angle close to the tinder bundle. Works wet. Carry one always.
  • Bow drill: Requires dry fireboard and spindle (willow, cottonwood, basswood, or cedar work well). Drill produces an ember in the notch; transfer ember to tinder bundle and blow gently. High skill requirement — practice before needing it.
  • Flint and steel: Strike high-carbon steel against sharp flint, quartz, or chert to shower sparks onto char cloth. Medieval reliable; difficult without char cloth.
  • Magnification: Convex lens (magnifying glass, glasses lens, water-filled clear bag, ice lens) focused to a pinpoint on dark tinder on a sunny day.
  • Battery + steel wool: Stretch fine steel wool and touch both terminals of a 9V battery to it. Blows gently. Works even with weak batteries.
  • Matches / lighter: Always carry waterproofed matches or a quality lighter as primary — improvised methods are backup.

Fire Lay Types

TypeConstructionBest For
TepeeLean kindling around tinder in a cone, leaving a door facing the windQuick starts; poor for cooking
Log cabinStack fuel in alternating square layers around tinder/kindling centerLong burn, coals for cooking
Star5 logs radiating from center like spokes; push inward as they burnEfficient fuel use; minimal splitting
TrenchNarrow trench aligned with wind; fire burns along trenchWindy conditions; concentrated heat for cooking
ReflectorStack green logs or rocks in a wall behind fireDirects heat toward shelter; dramatically increases warmth

Shelter

Priority and Hypothermia Risk

Exposure kills faster than thirst or hunger. You can die of hypothermia at 50°F (10°C) if wet and wind-exposed. Shelter is your first priority unless you are already dehydrated or injured.

The ground conducts heat away from your body 25× faster than still air. Always insulate yourself from the ground — a layer of dry leaves, branches, or a pack is mandatory before worrying about overhead cover.

Site Selection

  • Choose natural windbreaks — dense conifers, rock faces, hillsides. Wind doubles heat loss.
  • Avoid ridgelines, hilltops, valley bottoms (cold air pools), and dry riverbeds (flash floods).
  • Look for natural overhead cover: rock overhangs, fallen trees, dense conifers.
  • Proximity to water and fuel is helpful but not at the cost of a safe, sheltered location.
  • Check for widow-makers (dead branches overhead) and ant/wasp nests before settling.

Lean-to

Quickest structure. Lash a horizontal ridgepole between two trees at shoulder height. Lean branches densely against it at 45°. Layer bark, leaves, and debris from bottom to top like roof shingles (minimum 15 cm thick). Build a reflector fire in front for radiant heat. Open-fronted; poor in heavy rain or cold without a fire.

Debris Hut

Best no-fire shelter for cold weather. Lay a ridgepole from the ground to a tree fork or stump, elevated ~1 m at the fork. Lean sticks along both sides (ribs). Cover entirely with debris 60–90 cm thick — leaves, pine needles, bark, moss. Stuff the interior with dry leaves for bedding. Entry should be just large enough to squeeze into and be blocked with a leaf bundle.

A properly built debris hut can stay 15–20°C warmer than outside air with body heat alone. If you can't cover it with your arms spread wide, it needs more debris.

Snow Shelters

  • Snow trench: Dig a body-width trench, cover with branches/pack/skis topped with snow blocks. Quick and effective in open terrain.
  • Quinzhee: Pile snow into a 2 m mound, let it sinter 2 hours, then hollow it out to ~15 cm walls (insert sticks as thickness guides). Poke ventilation hole with a ski pole before sleeping.
  • Snow cave: Excavate into a stable drift. Sleeping platform should be raised above the entrance to trap warm air. Ventilation is critical — mark entrance with a pole in case of burial.

Emergency Warmth

  • Layer clothing to trap air. Wet clothing loses 90% of its insulation — change immediately if possible.
  • Cover the head — up to 40% of body heat loss occurs there.
  • Stuff dry leaves or grass inside clothing as improvised insulation.
  • Curl into a fetal position to minimize exposed surface area.
  • Two people sharing a sleeping bag doubles survival time in extreme cold.

Navigation

STOP First

Stop, Think, Observe, Plan. Moving while lost increases your search area exponentially and burns energy. Stop as soon as you suspect you're lost. Most rescues happen within 3 km of the last known point.

Solar Navigation

  • Sun arc: In the northern hemisphere, the sun rises roughly east, tracks south across the sky, and sets roughly west. Reverse in the southern hemisphere.
  • Shadow-stick method: Push a stick into flat ground. Mark the tip of the shadow. Wait 15 minutes. Mark again. A line from first to second mark runs roughly west to east. Stand with the first mark at your left foot and second at your right — you face north (northern hemisphere).
  • Watch compass (analog): Hold the watch flat. Point the hour hand at the sun. The bisecting angle between the hour hand and 12 o'clock is south (northern hemisphere). Adjust for daylight saving: use 1 o'clock instead of 12.

Stellar Navigation

  • Polaris (Northern Hemisphere): Find the Big Dipper's outer bowl edge. Extend a line 5× that distance — it points to Polaris (North Star). Polaris sits within ~1° of true north.
  • Southern Cross (Southern Hemisphere): Extend a line from the long axis of the Southern Cross 4.5× its length to find south. The two bright pointer stars outside the cross confirm orientation.

Terrain Reading

  • Water flows downhill to valleys and eventually to civilization. Following a stream downstream is the most reliable wilderness navigation method.
  • Ridgelines travel farther and faster than valleys but offer better visibility and signaling opportunities.
  • Drainage patterns (where streams join) help locate your position on a map.
  • Moss, lichen, and bark discoloration from moisture are unreliable compass indicators — use them as hints only.

Improvised Compass

Magnetize a needle using a reliable method: stroke it 50+ times in one direction along a permanent magnet (one end of a bar magnet or the pole of a compass magnet). Alternatively, use electrical induction — coil wire around the needle and connect briefly to a battery. Stroking against non-magnetic materials (silk, hair, clothing) does not reliably magnetize a needle and should not be relied on. Float the magnetized needle on a small leaf in still water or hang it from a thread. It will align north–south; confirm orientation using the shadow-stick or solar method.

Leaving a Trail

  • Cairns (stacked rocks) are universally understood trail markers.
  • Break branches at waist height pointing your direction of travel.
  • Scratch arrows into soft soil or bark at decision points.
  • Leave bright clothing scraps tied to trees at regular intervals.

Food & Foraging

Priorities

The body can survive 3 weeks without food — food is rarely the first priority unless the survival situation extends beyond a few days. Pursuing food at the expense of hydration or shelter is a fatal error. A resting adult burns roughly 1,500–2,000 calories/day; reduce activity to conserve this budget.

Do not eat if you have no water — digestion consumes water. Never eat unknown plants or fungi to "try them." Poisoning in a survival situation is catastrophically worse than going hungry.

Universal Edibility Test

Test one plant part at a time (leaf, root, seed) over an extended period. If any step causes reaction, discard that plant entirely.

  1. Fast 8 hours before testing.
  2. Crush a small piece; hold against inner wrist — wait 15 minutes. Check for redness, burning, or itching.
  3. Hold against outer lip 3 minutes.
  4. Hold against tongue tip 15 minutes.
  5. Chew a small amount thoroughly — hold in mouth 15 minutes without swallowing.
  6. Swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours. If no nausea, cramps, or numbness, eat a slightly larger amount and wait another 8 hours.
Even passing the full test is not a guarantee of safety — some toxins accumulate or only affect certain individuals. The test does not apply to mushrooms; do not eat wild fungi without expert identification.

General Foraging Rules

AvoidSafer Indicators
White or yellow berriesAggregate berries (raspberries, blackberries) — generally safe worldwide
Bitter or burning tasteCooking neutralizes many toxins — boil plants, discard water
Milky or discolored sapCattail (Typha) — entire plant is edible; one of the most reliable wild foods
Plants with umbrella-shaped flower clusters (many are deadly)Pine needles — edible, high in Vitamin C; boil for tea
Fine-haired caterpillarsDandelion — entire plant edible raw or cooked; worldwide distribution
Brightly colored insectsGrasshoppers / crickets — cook before eating; remove legs and wings

Insects

Insects are the most calorie-dense and abundant wild food source. They require minimal energy to collect and are high in protein and fat. Always cook insects to kill parasites and bacteria. Remove legs, wings, and heads (head may contain venom sacs). Beetles, ants, termites, and grasshoppers are the most globally available.

Trapping Basics

  • Snares: A wire loop (25–30 cm diameter for rabbit) suspended at neck height (a fist's width above the trail). Set on established game trails — look for tracks, droppings, or worn paths. Set 5–10 traps simultaneously to improve odds.
  • Deadfall (Paiute or figure-4): A heavy rock or log triggered to fall on prey. Requires careful carving and patience. Best for small mammals.
  • Check traps every few hours. Rotting bait attracts fewer animals and causes meat to spoil.

Fishing

  • Improvise hooks from pins, thorns, bent wire, or carved bone.
  • Any invertebrate (worms, insects, grubs) works as bait.
  • Fish are most active at dawn and dusk. Shallow edges and below fallen trees are prime locations.
  • A weir (stone funnel directing fish into a pocket) or a basket trap can be passive and highly effective in streams.

First Aid

This section is a quick reference. It does not replace Wilderness First Aid (WFA) or Wilderness First Responder (WFR) training. Seek professional certification.

Bleeding Control

  • Apply firm, direct pressure with the cleanest material available. Hold for 10 full minutes without lifting to check.
  • If blood soaks through, add more material on top — do not remove.
  • Tourniquet: Apply 5–8 cm above wound on a limb only (not the neck or torso). Tighten until bleeding stops. Note the time applied. A properly applied tourniquet is painful — that indicates correct tension. Do not remove in the field.
  • Wound packing: for deep wounds that cannot be controlled with surface pressure, pack cloth firmly into the wound cavity and apply sustained pressure.

Shock

Shock is inadequate blood/oxygen delivery to organs. It can follow bleeding, severe burns, allergic reaction, or infection. Signs: pale/cool/clammy skin; rapid, weak pulse; rapid shallow breathing; confusion or anxiety; nausea.

  • Lay the patient flat; elevate legs 15–20 cm (if no spinal injury suspected).
  • Keep them warm — a shocky patient loses heat rapidly.
  • Do not give food or water.
  • Treat the cause (control bleeding, stabilize fracture).
  • Seek evacuation immediately — shock is life-threatening.

Fractures

  • Stabilize the fracture by splinting in the position found — two rigid sticks padded with clothing either side of the limb, secured with strips of cloth above and below the break (not at the break).
  • Check pulse, sensation, and movement (PSM) distal to the injury before and after splinting.
  • Open fractures (bone through skin) require careful wound covering and expedited evacuation. Do not attempt to push bone back.
  • Spinal injury: do not move the patient unless they face immediate danger. If movement is unavoidable, maintain neutral spine alignment.

Hypothermia

StageCore TempSignsTreatment
Mild32–35°CShivering, impaired coordination, slurred speechRemove wet clothing, insulate, provide warm fluids if alert
Moderate28–32°CShivering stops, extreme drowsiness, confusionGentle handling, insulate, chemical heat packs to armpits/groin, no warm fluids
Severe<28°CUnconscious, barely palpable pulse, rigid musclesHandle extremely gently (risk of cardiac arrest), insulate, immediate evacuation
Warm the core first — not the extremities. Warming limbs first drives cold blood to the heart and can cause cardiac arrest (afterdrop).

Heat Illness

  • Heat cramps: painful muscle spasms during exercise. Rest in shade, drink electrolyte-containing water, gentle stretching.
  • Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, cool pale clammy skin, nausea, fainting. Move to shade, cool with wet cloths, elevate legs, encourage slow drinking.
  • Heat stroke: high body temp (40°C+), hot dry skin, confusion, unconsciousness. Medical emergency. Cool by any means — immersion, wet cloth with fanning, ice packs to neck/armpits/groin. Evacuate immediately.

Bites & Stings

  • Snake bite: Remain calm. Immobilize and keep bitten limb at or below heart level. Remove rings/tight items near the bite. Do not cut, suck, apply ice, or tourniquet. Evacuate immediately. Note snake appearance if safe to do so.
  • Anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction): Epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) if available, then evacuate. Signs: hives, swollen throat, difficulty breathing, rapid drop in blood pressure.
  • Tick removal: Grasp as close to skin as possible with fine-point tweezers. Pull straight upward with steady pressure. Do not twist. Clean with antiseptic.

Burns

  • Cool the burn with running water for 20 minutes. Do not use ice, butter, or toothpaste.
  • Cover with a clean, non-fluffy dressing. Do not pop blisters.
  • Burns covering >1% of body area (roughly the size of the patient's palm), or any burn to face/hands/genitals/joints, requires evacuation.

Signaling

The Universal Distress Signal

Three of anything — three whistle blasts, three shots, three fires in a triangle (30 m apart), three flashes of light. Pause, then repeat. This is universally recognized across cultures and aviation.

Ground-to-Air Signals

Construct symbols at least 10 meters long from rocks, logs, or trampled vegetation in a clearing, on snow, or on a beach. Make them as high-contrast as possible.

SymbolMeaning
XNeed medical assistance
VNeed help
→ (arrow)Traveling in this direction
YYes
NNo
LLAll is well

Signal Fire

  • Build in a clearing visible from the air. Have dry tinder ready — a signal fire you can't light quickly is useless.
  • Daytime: Add green leaves, wet moss, or rubber to create thick smoke. Smoke is visible from aircraft many kilometers away.
  • Nighttime: A bright flame with minimal smoke is most visible. Dry hardwood or petroleum-based materials.
  • Three fires in a triangle is the strongest signal — maintain fuel for all three.

Mirror Signaling

A signal mirror can be seen from over 15 km by aircraft on a sunny day. Aim: hold the mirror near your eye, extend your other hand in a V sign, aim the reflected sunspot through the V at the target aircraft. Flash repeatedly — even random reflection in a search area alerts rescuers.

Any reflective surface works: phone screen, foil, CD, watch face, polished metal. You don't need a purpose-built mirror.

Whistle

A plastic whistle carries far further than a human voice and requires a fraction of the energy. Blow three blasts, pause, repeat. A whistle is audible through dense forest where shouting is not. Always carry one.

Radio and Electronic Devices

  • PLB (Personal Locator Beacon): Single-use satellite distress alert — triggers a coordinated search and rescue response. No subscription required in most regions. The single most effective emergency device.
  • Satellite communicator (SPOT, Garmin inReach): Two-way messaging + tracking. Subscription required.
  • Cell phone: Try 911 / 112 even with no signal — emergency calls may connect on any available carrier. Move to high ground for better reception. Preserve battery by enabling airplane mode except when trying to signal.

Mindset

The Rule of Threes

This hierarchy defines your survival decision-making order:

TimeframeThreat
3 minutesWithout air (airway obstruction, drowning)
3 hoursIn harsh weather without adequate shelter (exposure)
3 daysWithout water
3 weeksWithout food

Always address the most immediate threat first. People die preparing fire when they should be building shelter, and search for food when they are already dangerously dehydrated.

STOP Protocol

The single most important procedure when lost or in danger:

  • Stop: Halt all movement immediately. Moving while uncertain dramatically expands your lost radius.
  • Think: What do I know? What was my last known position? What resources do I have? What are my immediate threats?
  • Observe: Read your environment. What's the weather doing? Where is water? What materials are available? Which direction is downhill?
  • Plan: Make a specific, simple plan with clear priorities. Announce it to companions. Revise it as conditions change.

Psychological Factors in Survival

Survival research — including studies of plane crash, shipwreck, and wilderness emergency survivors — consistently shows that the primary determinant of outcome is not physical fitness or technical skill: it is the will to live and the ability to think clearly under stress.

  • Fear is normal. Acknowledge it, then act. Paralysis is the danger, not the fear.
  • Small wins matter. Build a fire, collect water, construct a shelter — each task builds confidence and momentum.
  • Give yourself a reason to survive. Survivors frequently report fixating on a specific person, goal, or obligation. This is not weakness — it is a cognitive tool.
  • Avoid catastrophizing. Break the situation into the next manageable task. "I need to find water before dark" is actionable. "I'm going to die out here" is not.

Decision Making Under Stress

  • Stress hormones impair complex reasoning and amplify risk-taking. Slow your breathing (4 sec inhale, 4 hold, 4 exhale) to reduce cortisol and restore prefrontal function.
  • When considering an action, ask: "What is the worst case if this goes wrong, and can I recover from it?" Avoid irreversible actions under uncertainty.
  • Two people make better decisions than one — assign clear roles and decision points.
  • Fatigue severely impairs judgment. Prioritize sleep when the immediate threat is managed.

Training and Preparation

  • Take a certified Wilderness First Aid (WFA) or Wilderness First Responder (WFR) course.
  • Practice fire starting and shelter building before you need them — motor skills degrade under stress.
  • Always carry a basic kit: PLB or satellite communicator, fire starting, water purification, first aid, whistle, knife, map + compass.
  • File a detailed trip plan with a trusted contact and establish a check-in schedule before every trip into the backcountry.

Weather Hazards

Lightning

  • If you hear thunder, lightning is close enough to strike. Move into a substantial building or hard-topped vehicle.
  • In open country, leave ridges, summits, isolated trees, fences, and exposed water. Spread a group apart to reduce multiple casualties.
  • If no safer shelter exists, minimize your height and ground contact. Do not lie flat. This is a last resort, not a safe position.
  • Wait at least 30 minutes after the last thunder before resuming exposed activity.
Lightning victims do not retain an electrical charge. Call emergency services, begin CPR if needed, and treat burns or trauma.

Flash Floods

  • Move immediately to higher ground when water rises, turns muddy, carries debris, or you hear a distant roar.
  • Never camp in a dry wash, narrow canyon, or low river terrace when rain is possible upstream.
  • Do not walk or drive through moving floodwater. Depth, road damage, and current strength are difficult to judge.

Extreme Heat

Heat exhaustion may cause heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, headache, and dizziness. Stop exertion, move to shade, cool the person, and give small amounts of water if fully alert.

Heat stroke may cause confusion, collapse, seizures, or very hot skin. It is a medical emergency: call emergency services and cool rapidly with cold water, wet cloths, and airflow. Do not force fluids into a confused person.

Frostbite and Severe Cold

  • Protect exposed skin, add dry insulation, and block wind before numbness begins.
  • Frostbitten skin may be pale, waxy, hard, or numb. Remove constricting items and protect the area from pressure.
  • Do not rub frostbite or thaw it if refreezing is possible. Seek medical care; rapid rewarming requires controlled warm water and pain management.

Vehicle Survival

Stranded Vehicle Priorities

  1. Get out of traffic danger: stop as far from travel lanes as safely possible, use hazard lights, and remain belted if collision risk remains.
  2. Call or signal: share road, mile marker, GPS coordinates, direction of travel, vehicle description, occupants, and medical needs.
  3. Stay with the vehicle unless it is unsafe. A vehicle is easier to locate, provides shelter, and carries signaling materials.
  4. Manage exposure: add layers, shade windows in heat, conserve battery, and ration effort rather than water.

Cold Weather and Carbon Monoxide

  • Keep the exhaust pipe clear of snow before running the engine.
  • Run the engine only intermittently for heat, crack a downwind window, and check that exhaust is not entering the cabin.
  • Use bright cloth, reflective material, hazard lights, or a raised hood to signal rescuers.
Headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, or unusual sleepiness can indicate carbon monoxide poisoning. Shut off the engine, move to fresh air, and call for help.

Vehicle Emergency Kit

Carry water, shelf-stable food, first aid supplies, flashlight, power bank, warm layers, blanket, rain shell, reflective vest, warning triangles, tire inflator, basic tools, shovel, traction material, paper map, and seasonal supplies.

Essential Knots

These diagrams are for utility and shelter tasks, not climbing, rescue loads, or life safety. Practice with real cordage and inspect every knot before loading it.

Standing part Working end — both colors are the same rope.

Figure-Eight Stopper

Stops a rope end from pulling through a hole or another knot. Easy to inspect and untie.

  1. Make a loop.
  2. Pass the working end around the standing part.
  3. Feed it back through the original loop and dress into an “8”.
Finished check: one distinct figure-eight shape, no accidental extra turns, and the working end exits away from the standing part.

Bowline

Creates a fixed loop that does not tighten under ordinary load. Useful for shelter ridgelines and hauling gear.

  1. Form a small loop in the standing part.
  2. Bring the working end through it, around the standing part, and back through.
  3. Tighten while preserving a short tail; add a backup knot for critical utility loads.
Finished check: the tail is captured inside the small collar, the main loop stays fixed when loaded, and the tail is long enough to back up.

Taut-Line Hitch

An adjustable friction hitch for tent and tarp guylines. It slides when unloaded and grips when tensioned.

  1. Wrap the working end twice inside the loop around the standing line.
  2. Make one final wrap outside those turns.
  3. Dress neatly; slide to adjust, then load to grip.
Finished check: two adjacent turns are inside the guyline loop and one locking half hitch is outside them. It slides only when unloaded.

Clove Hitch

Fast attachment to a post or pole. Best when tension stays steady; back it up if the load may shift.

  1. Wrap the rope around the post.
  2. Cross over the standing part and wrap again.
  3. Tuck the working end under the crossing turn and pull both ends.
Finished check: two turns encircle the post, a single diagonal locking bar crosses them, and both ends emerge from the center in opposite directions.
